How to use this template

This is CandyCo's standard written response to a retail or brand customer about a consumer complaint investigation. It follows what GFSI-certified customers (Costco, Walmart, Kroger, and brand partners) expect to see: identification and traceability, containment, evidence-based investigation, a named root cause method, scope, disposition, and time-bound corrective actions with verification. Fill every yellow field, delete rows and blocks marked optional that don't apply, update the page numbers if you add or remove pages, and print to PDF (Letter, portrait, background graphics on, browser headers off).

Response timelines

MilestoneStandardNotes
Acknowledge receipt to the customerWithin 24 hoursEven one line: received, investigating, response date to follow.
Containment for critical / food safety complaintsWithin 24 hoursHold inventory, pull retains, distribution check — before further production where the line is implicated.
Full written investigation reportWithin 14 calendar daysOr the customer's stated SLA if shorter. Walmart specifies two weeks; Costco requires corrective action plans within 14 days.
If the investigation will run past the deadlineInterim updateNever go silent. Send what is known, what remains open, and a date.

Severity classification

ClassExamplesResponse
CriticalIllness or injury, medical attention, foreign object capable of harm, allergen mislabel or cross-contact, tampering, legal involvementEscalate to the VP of Food Safety & Quality immediately. MajorForeign material not capable of harm, off-flavor or odor, significant texture or appearance defect, packaging integrity failureFull investigation and CAPA using all sections of this template. Standard 14-day written response.
MinorCosmetic appearance (light bloom), minor fill or count variance, single-unit packaging cosmetic defectLog and trend. Investigation effort proportionate; a shortened response is acceptable. Repeat complaints on the same item escalate to major.

Confectionery-specific notes

Bloom (whitish or grey surface on chocolate) is a quality condition, not a safety issue — explain it in consumer-friendly terms, and where temperature abuse in distribution is the probable cause, present the evidence (shipping records, seasonal timing) rather than asserting it. For foreign material, the metal detection / X-ray CCP record is the evidence customers look for first. Treat every allergen complaint as critical until the investigation says otherwise — our facilities are peanut free, but milk, soy, wheat, egg, and tree nut are present by line.

Writing rules

DoDon't
Express concern without admitting cause: "We're sorry to hear about this experience" is safe.Admit liability or speculate about cause before the evidence supports it.
Stick to verified facts from the investigation record, with concrete numbers — lot codes, retain results, units produced, complaint counts.Pad with generalities. A response with no lot-specific evidence reads as boilerplate, and retailer FSQA reviewers flag it.
Name the root cause method and show the logic. If root cause was not confirmed, say so and present the most probable cause as probable.Write "root cause: operator error" or "unknown" with no analysis shown — and don't name or blame individuals.
Describe the process gap and the fix.Make "retrained employee" the only preventive action. Customers read that as no real fix.
Stay neutral if consumer fraud is suspected: "our investigation found no source of this material within our facility," with the basis.Blame the consumer or imply fraud, ever.
Use "isolated" only when complaint history genuinely supports it, and show the history.Call anything an "isolated incident" by default — misusing it destroys credibility on the next complaint.
Close decisively: state the investigation is closed and invite questions.Leave the conclusion ambiguous or the case open-ended.

Pre-send checklist

  • Every yellow field replaced; every guidance bracket deleted; both internal pages removed.
  • Page footers renumbered if pages were added or removed (customer report is Page N of 4 by default).
  • Lot code, item numbers, and dates match the complaint record and NetSuite lot trace exactly.
  • Containment, retained sample evaluation, and complaint history sections are all present — these are the first things a retailer FSQA reviewer looks for.
  • Every corrective action has an owner, a due date, and a verification step.
  • Severity is critical: VP of Food Safety & Quality has signed off; illness or injury: legal has reviewed.
  • A second quality reviewer has proofread the response.
  • Response is within the customer's SLA — or an interim update has already been sent.
  • Complaint logged and trended in the complaint register; CAPA entered in the CAPA log with the same reference number.
